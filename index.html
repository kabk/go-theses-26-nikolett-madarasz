Abstract

This research project explores how absence can become a form of presence through film. It began from a personal experience of losing my mother in 2024, where I found myself missing an essential role in my life. Two years later, grief feels different.

Focusing on grief, absence, and presence, the project does not represent grief directly. Instead, through objects, sounds, gestures, and daily repetition, it observes how presence can still be felt.

I investigate how ordinary elements such as a mug, a curtain, or the sound of a kettle can hold memory and evoke continued presence. The film is structured in two parts: a verbal and a non-verbal narrative. The first part is built from interviews with mothers and children, exploring relationships, care, and the role of motherhood. The second part shifts into silence, following a character moving through an empty home where absence becomes tangible through atmosphere, sound, and repetition.

By combining personal memory, staged environment, and collected voices, this work aims to transform individual loss into a shared emotional landscape, where viewers can recognize their own experiences within the presence of what is no longer physically there.
